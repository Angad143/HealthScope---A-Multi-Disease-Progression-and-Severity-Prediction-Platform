1. Diabetes

Description:

Diabetes is a condition where the body struggles to process sugar (glucose) from food. Normally, insulin helps move sugar from the blood into cells for energy. In diabetes, either the body doesn't produce enough insulin or can't use it effectively, leading to high blood sugar levels.
There are two main types: Type 1 diabetes (where the body doesn't produce insulin) and Type 2 diabetes (where the body doesn't use insulin properly). Over time, high blood sugar can lead to serious problems with our heart, eyes, kidneys, nerves, and more.

Common Symptoms:

  • 1. Frequent urination (especially at night)
  • 2. Increased thirst and hunger
  • 3. Unexplained weight loss
  • 4. Fatigue and irritability
  • 5. Blurred vision

Main Causes:

  • 1. Family history (genetics)
  • 2. Being overweight or obese
  • 3. Physical inactivity
  • 4. Unhealthy eating habits
  • 5. Age (risk increases as you get older)
  • 6. High blood pressure

Prevention Tips:

  • 1. Maintain a healthy weight through diet and exercise
  • 2. Eat balanced meals with plenty of fruits and vegetables
  • 3. Limit sugary foods and drinks
  • 4. Exercise regularly (at least 30 minutes daily)
  • 5. Get regular health checkups

Treatment Options:

  • 1. Regular blood sugar monitoring
  • 2. Insulin therapy (for Type 1 diabetes)
  • 3. Oral medications (for Type 2 diabetes)
  • 4. Healthy diet and exercise plan
  • 5. Regular doctor visits to monitor complications

2. Heart Disease

Description:

Heart disease includes various conditions that affect the heart, with coronary artery disease being the most common. It happens when arteries supplying blood to the heart narrow due to plaque buildup, reducing oxygen and nutrients.
This can cause chest pain, heart attacks, or worse. However, many forms of heart disease can be prevented or managed with healthy lifestyle choices.

Symptoms:

  • 1. Chest pain, tightness, or discomfort (angina)
  • 2. Shortness of breath
  • 3. Pain in neck, jaw, throat, upper abdomen or back
  • 4. Nausea, fatigue, or lightheadedness
  • 5. Irregular heartbeat
  • 6. Swelling in legs, ankles or feet

Main Causes:

  • 1. High blood pressure
  • 2. High cholesterol levels
  • 3. Smoking and tobacco use
  • 4. Unhealthy diet
  • 5. Excessive alcohol use

Prevention Tips:

  • 1. Don't smoke or use tobacco
  • 2. Exercise for at least 30 minutes most days
  • 3. Eat a heart-healthy diet (fruits, vegetables, whole grains)
  • 3. Maintain a healthy weight
  • 4. Get regular health screenings

Treatment Options:

  • 1. Lifestyle changes (diet, exercise, quitting smoking)
  • 2. Medications to control blood pressure, cholesterol, etc.
  • 3. Medical procedures like angioplasty or bypass surgery
  • 4. Regular monitoring by a cardiologist

3. Kidney Disease

Description:

Kidney disease occurs when your kidneys are damaged and can't filter blood effectively. These bean-shaped organs remove waste, balance minerals, make urine, and regulate blood pressure. Chronic kidney disease (CKD) means lasting damage that can get worse over time. If the damage is very bad, your kidneys may stop working completely.
This is called kidney failure, and you would need dialysis or a kidney transplant to survive. Many people don't realize they have kidney disease until it's advanced because symptoms often don't appear until the kidneys are badly damaged.

Symptoms:

  • 1. Fatigue and weakness
  • 2. Shortness of breath
  • 3. Blood in urine (may appear pink or cola-colored)
  • 4. Puffy eyes, especially in the morning
  • 5. Dry, itchy skin

Main Causes:

  • 1. Diabetes (leading cause of kidney disease)
  • 2. High blood pressure
  • 3. Glomerulonephritis (inflammation of kidney filters)
  • 4. Polycystic kidney disease (inherited condition)
  • 5. Prolonged urinary tract obstruction

Prevention Tips:

  • 1. Manage high blood pressure
  • 2. Eat a balanced, low-salt diet
  • 3. Stay hydrated by drinking enough water
  • 4. Don't smoke or use tobacco products
  • 5. Get regular exercise

Treatment Options:

  • 1. Medications to control underlying causes
  • 2. Dietary changes to reduce kidney workload
  • 3. Treatment for complications like anemia or weak bones
  • 4. Regular monitoring of kidney function
